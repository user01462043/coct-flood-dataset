Background

About the database

The City of Cape Town Flood Events Database brings together reported flood occurrences from multiple sources to support analysis of flooding patterns across the city between 2015 and 2024.

For the purposes of this database, a flood event is defined as the temporary inundation of areas not normally covered by water, caused by overflow of a stream, canal or other water body, or by the accumulation of surface or sea water, resulting in observable disruption such as inundated dwellings or roads, evacuations, or service interruptions.

This definition covers fluvial, pluvial and coastal events. Isolated burst pipes and plumbing leaks are excluded unless they are associated with a wider flooding event.

Each record represents a reported flood occurrence and includes information such as date, location, source, reported impacts and flood characteristics where available. Spatial standardisation procedures were applied so that locations can be compared consistently across sources.

The database was developed as part of the CASCADE project to support flood-risk research, data exploration and improved understanding of how reported flood impacts are distributed across the city.

Validation and cleaning

Data validation, cleaning and spatial standardisation

Flood-impact validation

To assess the reliability of the AI-assisted extraction workflow, a verification and validation exercise focused on impact-class assignments, identified event locations and extracted event dates.

Reviewer consensus

Source documents were processed by two authors, producing independent extraction outputs. Discrepancies were discussed and resolved through consensus.

Spatial standardisation

Reported locations were reviewed and standardised against a common place-name reference. Where possible, locations were matched to Main Place records to support consistent mapping.

Spatial standardisation note: place names across sources often used alternative spellings, abbreviations or different geographic detail. This harmonisation step supports comparison across sources and mapping within the City of Cape Town municipal boundary.

Limitations and responsible use

How users should interpret the database

Incomplete and potentially biased reporting

No single source captures all flood occurrences. EM-DAT mainly records large disasters, SAWS summaries focus on noteworthy weather events, FloodList and news reports depend on public reporting, and municipal service requests only capture events reported to the municipality. Some events may therefore be absent.

Variable spatial precision

Some records refer to specific suburbs or neighbourhoods, while others are reported at broader spatial scales such as Cape Town, Western Cape or other administrative areas. This introduces uncertainty when interpreting hotspots and spatial patterns.

Uneven impact information

Not all sources provide the same level of detail. EM-DAT and municipal service requests may confirm occurrence but contain limited local impact information, while SAWS, FloodList and news articles often provide richer descriptions.

Dependence on reported descriptions

Impact classifications were derived from the information available in source documents. Where descriptions were limited, vague or incomplete, assigned classifications may under-represent the full extent or severity of actual impacts.

Example applications

What the database can be used for

The database is intended as a research and exploration resource. It can support several forms of flood-risk analysis when used with appropriate caution and source-awareness.

Flood hotspot mapping

Identify places with recurrent reported flood impacts.

Flood frequency analysis

Explore how reported flood records vary over time.

Impact severity assessment

Compare the types and severity of reported flood consequences.

Rainfall–flood comparison

Compare reported flood dates with rainfall observations or thresholds.

Municipal planning

Support flood-risk discussion, planning and prioritisation.
